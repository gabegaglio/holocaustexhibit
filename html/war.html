Freida Radasky (1918-1999)

Freida Radasky was born in Warsaw, Poland, as Franya Boorstein on April 15th of 1918, the youngest of seven siblings. She was forced to hide in cellars when Warsaw was bombed in 1939 and developed very poor eyesight from this. After leaving this cellar, she was forced to work in a kitchen by a coal mine. During the war many of her experiences included:

  • Being imprisoned at Paviak jail.
  • Carrying loads of brick.
  • Forced to create shoes out of straw.
  • Working for wealthy German families.

    • She was forced to witness German soldiers, at the Umschlagplatz in Warsaw, shoot her sister, after she had refused to give up her 4 year old son to the soldiers. After surviving the war, she married in 1948 in Germany, had her first son David, and moved to New Orleans to reunite with two of her brothers.

    Paulina Braun (1918-1999)

    Paulina Braun in 1915, was a songwriter and composer of the Warsaw Ghetto. Before she was forced into the ghetto, she had made a name for herself in the Warsaw theatre scene, and wrote musical theatre songs in both Yiddish and Polish. Once Paulina was placed into the Warsaw Ghetto, along with over 100,000 other Jews, her musical style shifted to songs of expressing pain and suffering, specifically regarding the horrendous conditions of the ghetto, such as starvation and disease. Specifically, she emphasized the suffering of mothers and their families, highlighting the maternal strife in the Warsaw Ghetto. Paulina also worked with and wrote songs for the most famous alto singer in the Warsaw Ghetto, Diana Blumenfeld. One of her most memorable and meaningful songs is 'Hots Rakhmones', written in Yiddish, translating to 'Have Compassion, Jewish Hearts' in English. Braun was deported from the Warsaw Ghetto to the Majdanek concentration camp in Lublin, Poland, where she tragically died in November, 1943.

    The ghetto wrestles with death,
    People are little more than shadows,

    You see people running, making a racket in the streets,

    Eyes shine out of a pale face,

    I still want to live, I still want to see the world!

    Throw down a piece of dry bread!

    And even when you do not want to hear,—

    Have compassion, Jewish hearts,
    I am a common man just like you!

    "Ani Ma'amin"

    “Ani Ma’amin” (which means “I believe”) was the song of the religious Jews in the Warsaw Ghetto. It is a rendition of Maimonides' thirteen-point version of the Jewish principles of faith. It was sung as an anthem of hope and allowed many Jews to keep their belief while they were in the German-built ghettos, concentration camps, and death camps during World War II.

    In the bunkers Jews are singing: “ani ma'amin—I believe In the coming of the Messiah, even though, even though he may tarry, I believe”— He will come, from there and from here.

    Jews are singing in a camp: “I believe— I believe, I believe, even though,” That even though he may tarry, I believe— I believe like a believer, a DP [displaced person].

    Let’s all sing now: “I believe”— If we do not sing it—it will sing itself anyway. Both at evening and at dawn, I believe, “Even though he may tarry, even though...”

    He will come, he must come—“I believe.” Don’t ask anyone, don’t ask when and how. Jews are singing: “I believe, I believe”— Look, he is coming, look, he is already here.
